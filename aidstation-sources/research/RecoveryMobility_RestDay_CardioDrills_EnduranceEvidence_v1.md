# Recovery/Mobility dose · Rest-day composition · Cardio drills-vs-volume — endurance-athlete evidence synthesis

**Date:** 2026-06-17
**Purpose:** Evidence base for two Layer-4 design tracks Andy directed (2026-06-17, #698 thread): (1) a deterministic **recovery/mobility session** allocation alongside cardio/strength/rest (incl. the full-rest-vs-active-recovery rest-day question), and (2) a **cardio drills "consider these" pool** wired into cardio prescription (incl. how much to lean on structured drills vs plain volume). Differentiator #6: science-backed, cited.
**Method:** 5 parallel web-research agents (recovery/mobility dose · flexibility/mobility efficacy · rest-day full-vs-active · drills-vs-volume · periodization), peer-reviewed/systematic-review-first, skeptical of weak literature. Claims below carry their source + a confidence tag. Several claims corroborated independently across two agents (noted).

---

## 1. Recovery & mobility — does it work, and how much?

**Efficacy (skeptical pass) — mostly small/null for performance & injury:**
- Stretching has **no significant effect on running economy** (2025 SR+MA, 15 studies/40 ES: ES=0.25, 95% CI −0.16–0.66, p=0.21; 6 studies null). Some dynamic-stretch protocols *increased* energy cost. `pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12122984/` — **moderate confidence.**
- Foam rolling: **reliable acute ROM gain** (d=0.76, 95% CI 0.55–0.98 `pubmed/32825976`; chronic >4wk ES≈0.82 `PMC9474417`) but **performance effects small-to-negligible** (pre-roll sprint +0.7%/g=0.28, jump −1.9%, strength +1.8%; `PMC6465761`, **cited by two agents**). Largest recovery effect is on **perceived** muscle pain (+6.0%, g=0.47) — partly placebo (studies unblindable). **Moderate confidence.**
- Foam rolling gives **no flexibility benefit over other warm-ups** (2024 MA, 38 studies) `fitchef.com/...` — *secondary source, verify before quoting* — **low confidence.**
- Injury prevention: 2025 Delphi consensus (20 researchers) **does not recommend stretching for injury prevention**; static stretching may cut muscle injuries but with a possible **bone/joint injury trade-off**; **strength/stability/postural-control programs are better-supported**. `PMC12305623` — **contested / low-moderate confidence.**

**Dose (where numbers exist — mostly flexibility/ROM, not endurance perf):**
- ACSM: flexibility **≥2–3 days/wk**, holds 10–30 s, ~60 s total per muscle group. `ptpioneer.com/...ACSM` — **practitioner/guideline.**
- Flexibility gains **plateau at ~4 min/session (acute) and ~10 min/week per muscle group (chronic)**; intensity/frequency/age/sex don't moderate (Warneke et al. 2024 SR/meta-regression). `sciencedirect.com/.../S2095254625000468` — **high confidence (for ROM outcome).**
- 2025 Delphi: ~60 s/muscle for warm-up; **3×120 s** static/PNF for chronic ROM. `PMC12305623` — **moderate.**
- ROM **decays in 2–6 wk** without maintenance → some ongoing work needed. `PMC12638565` — **moderate.**
- Breathwork: slow breathing raises vagal HRV during practice (RMSSD SMD≈0.37) with **small carry-over** (g≈0.14–0.32); **~6 breaths/min** ("resonance") maximizes HRV; **5 min ≈ as good as 20 min acutely**; lasting resting shift needs **~3 months** regular practice. `S0149763422002007`, `PMC5709795`, `pubmed/34886206` — **moderate.**
- Activation/priming: **no validated weekly dose**; priming can speed VO2 kinetics but findings conflict, highly intensity/recovery/individual-sensitive. `PMC10115720` — **low confidence.**

**→ Design implication:** recovery/mobility is a **modest, capped** allocation — small doses clear the evidence-supported benefit (ROM maintenance, perceived recovery, HRV); piling on volume buys nothing. Don't build a big weekly recovery block. ~2–3 short touches/week (≈10–15 min) is consistent with the dose-plateau data. Mobility/stretch is **not** an injury-prevention lever — that's what the strength pool is for.

## 2. Rest days — full rest vs active recovery

- **Long-term adaptation: active vs passive is INCONCLUSIVE** — both viable (2024 SR, 24 studies). `PMC10914654` — **the key finding; corroborated by the HIIT-mesocycle null `PMC5916096`.** — **high confidence in the "inconclusive" verdict.**
- Active recovery **clears lactate faster** (best ≈60–80% of lactate threshold), but that's an **acute, intra-session** effect, not a rest-day adaptation effect. `eprints.gla.ac.uk/95848` — **high.**
- **Within** a HIIT session, **passive** recovery preserves interval quality better in trained athletes. `PMC10914654` — **moderate.**
- 2-wk SIT: low-moderate active recovery raised critical power +7.9% vs no change passive — but a 4-wk HIIT block showed **no between-group difference**. So mode effects are protocol-specific, not general. `PMC6445608`, `PMC5916096` — **moderate.**
- **Full/complete rest is reserved for confirmed overtraining syndrome** (recovery 6 mo–2 yr, consensus); non-functional overreaching → **~50% volume cut** as the *minimum*, not total rest; functional overreaching resolves in **1–2 wk**. `PMC10409951`, `runstrong.co.uk/...` — **moderate (OTS strong; routine-fatigue prescriptions weaker).**
- Deloads: typically **every 4–6 wk for 5–7 days**, by cutting volume (often a few light sessions, not total time off). `frontiersin.org/.../1073223` — **practitioner.**
- Practical consensus (**expert opinion, not RCT**): **≥1 full rest day + ≥1 light/active day per week**, deload every **3–4 wk**. `medichecks.com/...` — **low (expert opinion).**

**→ Design implication:** the science **does not mandate full rest** for a healthy athlete — light active recovery and full rest are **both defensible**, and adaptation doesn't hinge on the choice. So make rest days **load-adaptive**: default to genuine **full rest** for the protective floor, but allow a **light active-recovery variant** (easy movement / mobility / breathwork). Bias toward **full rest when accumulated load/fatigue is high** (deload weeks, post-peak, illness flags). This is a coaching-judgment surface — a good fit for "deterministic floor + LLM nuance."

## 3. Cardio drills vs plain volume — and which disciplines

**Discipline split is the headline:**
- **Swimming: technique dominates** (performance is drag-limited; drag coefficient falls as technical efficiency rises; even 4 wk technical work cut active drag). **Drills transfer strongly.** `frontiersin.org/.../938658`, `PMC3988165` (cutting volume ~50% while adding intensity didn't worsen 100/200m → mileage isn't the lever) — **moderate-high.**
- **Running: form/gait drills change biomechanics but NOT performance/economy.** Gait-retraining MA (19 trials/673): raised cadence, cut loading rate, **no performance effect**; footstrike retraining **null** on economy; cadence↔economy correlation **trivial** (r≈−0.09–0.11). **Strength & plyometrics transfer better** to running economy than drills. `jospt.org/.../10585`, `pubmed/29100250`, `PMC11127892`, `PMC11052887` — **moderate-high (equivocal/null is the robust finding).**
- **Cycling: pedalling-technique drills weak-to-negative on efficiency** ("pull up" raised force effectiveness but **lowered** gross efficiency; cadence strongly coupled to energy cost). `PMC3218268` — **moderate.**
- Running cadence cueing is justified as an **injury/biomechanics** tool, **not** a performance/economy lever. `jospt.org/.../10585` — **moderate.**
- **No RCT quantifies optimal drill frequency/dose** in any discipline — it's inferred. — **explicit gap.**

**Periodization (when drills matter):**
- Polarized **~80/20** (75–85% LIT / 17–26% HIT); distribution becomes **more polarized** toward competition (Seiler). `sportsci.org/2009/ss.htm`, `PMC11679080` — **high.**
- Taper: **≤21 days, cut volume 41–60%, maintain intensity & frequency** (2023 PLOS One MA; SMD −0.77). `journals.plos.org/.../pone.0282838` — **high.**
- **Skill/drill work is front-loaded in Base and progressively displaced by race-specific intensity toward Build/Peak/taper** (Friel; strength likewise de-emphasized toward racing). `joefrieltraining.com/...`, `trainingpeaks.com/...` — **practitioner.**
- No peer-reviewed "% of weekly time that is non-primary" figure exists. — **gap.**

**→ Design implication:** keep cardio **LLM-composed** but feed a **discipline-weighted "consider these drills" pool**: weight **heavily** for technical disciplines (swimming, paddling, technical MTB, skiing) and **lightly** for steady running/cycling (where volume + strength beat drills). **Periodize** the emphasis: drills prominent in **Base**, tapering off through **Build→Peak**. Don't over-prescribe running/cycling form drills — the evidence says they don't move performance.

## 4. Net design parameters (evidence-derived, for the /plan)
1. **Recovery/mobility:** small capped allocation, ~2–3 short sessions/wk (~10–15 min); a dedicated session **kind** for tracking, but low-volume. Phase: hold steady/slightly down toward taper. **Not** an injury lever.
2. **Rest day:** load-adaptive — full-rest default (protective floor), active-recovery variant allowed; bias to full rest under high load/deload. Adaptation is mode-agnostic, so this is a judgment surface.
3. **Cardio drills:** discipline-weighted pool (swimming/technical ≫ running/cycling), Base-emphasized, Peak/taper-minimized; LLM composes, catalog grounds.

## 5. Evidence-quality caveats
- Strongest: stretching dose-plateau, polarized/taper numbers, active-vs-passive "inconclusive," swimming-technique-matters, running-drills-don't-move-economy.
- Weakest/contested: stretching↔injury, foam-rolling-over-other-warmups (secondary src), activation/priming dose, rest-day/deload exact frequencies (expert opinion), optimal drill dose (no RCT).
- Two figures need primary-source confirmation before any user-facing copy quotes them: the 38-study foam-rolling-vs-warmup MA (claim via secondary mirror) and the NSCA taper 60–90% volume-cut (search snippet).
